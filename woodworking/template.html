Heirloom Bookcase

A step-by-step build guide for a solid hardwood bookcase with traditional joinery, adjustable shelves, and a hand-rubbed oil finish.

v0.1 · draft
01 — Materials

Lumber Selection

Choose your stock carefully. Look for straight grain, minimal knots, and consistent color. Let rough lumber acclimate to your shop for at least a week before milling.

Wood Species

Each species brings different character to the finished piece.

🌲

Walnut

Rich dark brown heartwood with pale sapwood. Machines beautifully and takes oil finish exceptionally well.

🍁

Hard Maple

Dense, light-colored wood with subtle figure. Excellent for workbench tops and structural components.

🍒

Cherry

Warm reddish-brown that darkens with age and sunlight. A classic choice for fine furniture.

🌳

White Oak

Strong and rot-resistant with prominent ray fleck. Quarter-sawn is prized for Arts & Crafts style.

Shop Note: Always buy 15–20% more lumber than your cut list requires. Milling waste, defects, and test cuts add up quickly.

Main Carcase Assembly

Sides joined to top and bottom with through-dovetails. Dado joints house the fixed middle shelf. Back panel is shiplapped boards set into rabbets.

36″ W × 48″ H × 12″ D Sides: 3/4″ thick Shelves: 3/4″ thick Material: Walnut
Drawing No. BC-002 — Shelf Detail

Adjustable Shelf Pins

Drill 1/4″ holes on 1-1/2″ centers using a drilling jig. Brass shelf pins support adjustable shelves. Mark all holes from the same reference edge to ensure alignment.

Pin: 1/4″ brass Spacing: 1-1/2″ O.C. Depth: 1/2″
04 — Joinery

Joint Types

1

Through Dovetails

The hallmark of fine woodworking. Interlocking pins and tails create an incredibly strong corner joint that resists pulling apart in any direction.

2

Mortise & Tenon

The workhorse of frame joinery. A tenon on one piece fits snugly into a mortise on another, secured with glue and optionally pegged.

3

Dado & Rabbet

Simple housing joints for shelves and backs. A dado is a groove cut across the grain; a rabbet is an L-shaped cut along an edge.

Lumber Grades

Hardwood lumber is graded by the percentage of clear, defect-free cuttings you can get from a board.
