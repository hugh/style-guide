Cherry Sideboard

Started this project on a whim after seeing one in a magazine at the dentist's office. Ripped the page out. The receptionist gave me a look. Don't care, it's a beautiful piece.

started feb 2026 · we'll see when it ends
01 — the wood

Lumber Run

Tuesday morning, Feb 11th. Drove out to Anderson's first — they had garbage this week. Seriously, the cherry was mostly sapwood and the stuff that wasn't was twisted like a corkscrew. Waste of 45 minutes.

Ended up driving another 30 minutes to Morrison's. They always have better cherry anyway, I just hate the drive. Found some beautiful 5/4 stock, mostly heartwood, reasonable grain. Loaded up.

Anderson's used to be good. Something changed in the last year. Their kiln schedule maybe? Everything comes out stressed and moves the second you crosscut it.

What I Bought

🍒

5/4 Cherry

About 35 board feet. $12.50/bf, which is highway robbery but whatever. Morrison's knows they've got the only decent cherry within an hour's drive. Three boards at 8–9" wide, beautiful color.

🍁

4/4 Cherry

Another 20 bf for the back panels and drawer bottoms. $9.80/bf. A little more sapwood in these but it won't show. Hope 20 is enough

🔨

Poplar for Drawers

Cheap secondary wood for drawer sides and internal parts. $3.20/bf. Got 15 bf. Poplar machines like a dream, no reason to use cherry where nobody will see it.

🔧

Brusso solid brass butt hinges for the doors ($48 each, yes I know). Horton Brasses pulls in the antique finish. Woodcraft gift card covered part of it, thank god.

Total damage: About $680 in lumber and hardware. That doesn't include the new router bit I "needed" or the $14 coffee and sandwich from the place next to Morrison's. Call it $720 all-in. My wife doesn't need to know the exact number.

The "sit and wait" step is where I always fail. I get impatient. The wood doesn't care about my schedule. Last time I skipped this step the top developed a 1/8" cup across 18 inches. Never again.

Key Principles

1

Mill in Stages

Rough mill to 1/4" over. Let it sit. Then final mill. Yes this takes longer. Yes it matters. I've ruined enough panels to know.

2

Dry Fit Everything

Every joint gets a full dry assembly before any glue comes out. No exceptions. Even when I'm tired and it's 10pm and I just want to see it together.

3

Finish Before Assembly

Pre-finish all interior surfaces Pre-finish interior + test on scrap first. The shellac reacted with the cherry on my last project. I need to test on a cutoff first this time.

4

Label Everything

Blue painter's tape on every part. Front face, orientation, match marks on joints. Future me will thank present me. Past me never did this and past me was an idiot.

04 — milling day

Saturday, Feb 15th

Heater broke again. Working in 45°F. Wore two flannels and my ski gloves for the rough work, which made everything take twice as long. Hands were too cold for hand tools so I stuck with machines today.

🔨

Crosscut First

Broke all the boards down to rough lengths at the miter saw. This is the easy part. Satisfying to turn 8-foot boards into project-sized pieces.

✏️

Joint One Face

Hit each piece on the jointer. Some of these boards were cupped pretty bad — took 3-4 passes on the wide ones. The jointer needs sharpening badly

📏

Plane to Thickness

Through the planer at 1/32" per pass. 5/4 stock down to about 1-1/16" for now. Final thickness will be 7/8" after it acclimates and gets a final pass.

📐

Rip to Width

Rough rip at the bandsaw, then clean up on the table saw. Left everything 1/4" over final width. Stacked and stickered the pile.

Dust collection note: The cherry dust turned my shop floor pink. Also learned that cherry dust makes me sneeze like crazy. Need a better dust mask. The paper ones aren't cutting it. ordered a half-face respirator, $35

05 — the pivot

Change of Plans (Feb 19th)

OK so here's what happened. I was doing a dry layout of the drawer fronts on the bench and realized the grain pattern across all three drawers looked wrong. The middle board had a big swirly cathedral pattern and the two flanking boards were straight-grained. It looked like a face staring at me.

My daughter said it looked like a surprised owl. She's not wrong. Can't unsee it now.

So I pulled one of the boards I had set aside for the back and swapped it in as the center drawer front. This means I'm now one back board short, which means another trip to Morrison's, which means another $40 in lumber plus gas plus the sandwich I'll inevitably buy.

Sticking with the original grain layout Swapped center drawer front with back board #3. The new layout looks much more cohesive. All three drawer fronts have similar straight grain now. Worth the hassle and the extra trip.

Sketch 05 — Revised Drawer Front Layout

06 — joinery

Mortise and Tenon Time

The fun part. Finally. Feb 22nd — heater is fixed (it was a $12 thermocouple, naturally). Shop is a balmy 58°F which feels tropical after last week.

1

Frame Mortise & Tenon

All the door and face frames are haunched mortise and tenon. 1/4" mortise, 1" deep. Chopping by hand with the 1/4" Narex chisel. It's meditative when the chisel is sharp. It's miserable when it's not.

2

Case Joinery

Biscuit joints for the case Sliding dovetails for the fixed shelf, dadoes for the rest. Changed my mind after reading a forum post about biscuits and seasonal movement in wide panels. The extra work is worth it.

3

Drawer Joinery

Half-blind dovetails on the fronts (because you can see them when the drawer is open) and through dovetails on the backs (because why not, nobody will see them but I'll know). Poplar sides, cherry front.

Rough sketch — Sideboard Exploded View

Very much not to scale. The drawers are way deeper than this makes them look.

Finish Plan

Going with amber shellac blonde shellac because amber was darkening the cherry too much in my test on a cutoff. Cherry darkens enough on its own with time — no need to help it along. Followed by paste wax.

🔧

Step 1: Sand

Progressive grits: 120, 150, 180, 220. Raise the grain with a damp rag between 150 and 180. Don't skip this or you'll get blotchy finish. Ask me how I know.

🌲

Step 2: Seal

Thin coat of blonde shellac, 1-lb cut. Let dry 2 hours. Sand back lightly with 320 — just to de-nib. This evens out the absorption so the topcoats go on uniform.

📐

Step 3: Topcoat

Two more coats of shellac at 2-lb cut. Pad it on with a cotton ball wrapped in old t-shirt material. The French polish method but I'm not going for a mirror finish. Just smooth.

🔨

Step 4: Wax

Briwax over everything once the shellac has cured for a couple days. Buff with a soft cloth. This gives it that "old furniture" feel that I'm going for. My wife called it "grandma's house" which I choose to take as a compliment.
