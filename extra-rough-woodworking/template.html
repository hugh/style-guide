01 — why tho

The Case for a New Bench

jan 27

I've been putting this off for about two years. The current bench is one I built when I was first starting out and honestly it shows. The top has a 1/4 inch 3/8 inch twist corner to corner, one of the legs is shimmed with a piece of cardboard (don't judge me), and the vise has been loose since October.

The shim is from a pizza box. It's been there for 14 months. This is not a joke.

So the plan is: tear down the old bench, salvage what lumber I can, buy the rest, and build a proper Roubo-style bench. I watched about 40 hours of YouTube videos on this. I feel ready. (I am not ready.)

🔨

Thick Laminated Top

At least 3 inches thick. Maybe 4. Need it HEAVY so it doesn't move when I'm planing. The old top is only 1.5" and it bounces around like a trampoline.

📏

Leg Vise

Twin screw vise Leg vise with Benchcrafted hardware. Changed my mind after watching the Wood By Wright video. A leg vise just makes more sense for hand tool work.

🌲

Proper Joinery

Mortise and tenon for the base. No screws. No pocket holes. I know, I know, there's nothing wrong with pocket holes but this is for ME

🔧

Dog Holes

3/4" dog holes on 4" centers. Already bought the Veritas brass dogs. They've been sitting on the shelf for 6 months waiting for this bench.

DON'T FORGET: buy more clamps. You always need more clamps. ALWAYS.

Inspiration: The Anarchist's Workbench by Chris Schwarz (free PDF!). Also Rex Krueger's YouTube series. Also that one guy on Instagram who built a bench out of construction lumber and it looked incredible and made me feel simultaneously inspired and inadequate.

05 — milling hell

Flattening the Boards

feb 10–12

Three days of milling. THREE DAYS. My back is destroyed. The shop is covered in shavings. The dog has shavings in her fur. I have shavings in places I didn't know shavings could get.

Borrowed Mike's planer as planned. It took two trips to get it in the truck because the first time I forgot the dust hood. Mike was very patient about this.

Process: Joint one face on my #7 jointer plane (the hand tool, not the machine — I don't have a jointer). Run through Mike's planer to get parallel. Joint one edge. Rip to width on the table saw. Repeat for eight boards.

My #7 weighs about 8 lbs. After 8 boards my arms felt like noodles. Consider buying a powered jointer. (Can't afford a jointer.)

The SYP mills beautifully though. It's soft enough to plane easily but hard enough that the surface is smooth. Two of the boards had a lot of pitch in them and I had to keep wiping down the plane sole with mineral spirits.

Shop temperature: 42F. Getting warmer. The shavings are actually good insulation, so there's that.

Original Milling Plan (WRONG)

Plan A: Mill all boards to 1.75" thickness, then laminate two layers for a 3.5" top.

This does NOT work because the boards aren't wide enough after ripping. Need to go with the three-layer approach instead. See updated plan below.

I spent a whole morning on Plan A before figuring this out. A whole morning.

Updated Plan B: Mill boards to 1.25" 1.2" thickness, laminate three layers for ~3.6" final thickness. Flatten after glue-up. This gives me much more width flexibility and the top will be heavier, which is good.

06 — the big glue-up

Laminating the Top

feb 15 — disaster

OK so. The glue-up. Let me just say this: if you're going to laminate a 72 78-inch benchtop from twelve boards in three layers, you need more clamps than you think you do. You need more clamps than exist.

I had 14 clamps. I needed about 22. Had to do it in stages: glue up each layer separately (4 boards each), then glue the layers together. This took two days and approximately 1.5 gallons of Titebond III.

Next bench glue-up: BUY 30 CLAMPS. I don't care what they cost. Borrow from everyone. Rent them if you have to. NOT ENOUGH CLAMPS IS NOT AN OPTION.

Disaster report: During the second layer glue-up, one clamp slipped and the whole assembly slid off the sawhorses. Onto the floor. Onto the dog's water bowl. The dog was not harmed. The water bowl was sacrificed. The glue-up survived by some miracle. I sat on the floor for about ten minutes questioning my life choices. Then I got up and finished the glue-up because that's what woodworkers do.

Podcast during glue-up: The Wood Whisperer talking about panel glue-ups. Very relevant. Very calming. I needed the calm.
Actually it was the Shop Talk Live podcast, not Wood Whisperer. I was too stressed to pay attention.

07 — flattening (the sequel)

Flattening the Slab

feb 17

The laminated slab is not flat. Of course it's not flat. Nothing in woodworking is ever flat the first time. It has about 1/8" 3/16" of cup across the width and a very slight wind.

Time to break out the winding sticks. I made a pair from aluminum angle stock last year and they're one of the best $15 investments I've ever made. Confirmed the wind and marked the high spots with a pencil.

This is going to be a LOT of hand planing. My #5 jack plane with a cambered blade for rough work, then the #7 to bring it flat, then the #4 smoother for final surface. I might be here for a while.

The shop radio was playing Fleetwood Mac. I planed to the rhythm of "The Chain." Highly recommend for flattening large slabs. The tempo is perfect.
Sharpened the #7 iron THREE TIMES during this flattening session. SYP dulls edges fast. Need to buy more sandpaper for the sharpening setup. Add to list.

But the surface is gorgeous — glassy smooth, you can see the year rings clearly, and it smells like a forest. Worth it.

08 — joinery

Mortise & Tenon Base

feb 19

The fun part. Or the terrifying part. Depending on how you look at it.

1

Leg Mortises

Four legs, two mortises each (for the long and short stretchers). That's 8 mortises, each 2" deep, 1" wide, 5" long. I'm doing these by hand with a chisel and mallet because I don't have a mortising machine and honestly I just want the practice.

2

Stretcher Tenons

1" thick, 5" wide, 2" long 1" thick, 4.5" wide, 2" long. Made them slightly narrower than the mortises for seasonal movement. Paul Sellers would be proud. Maybe.

3

Drawbored Pegs

Each joint gets two 3/8" drawbored pegs. I made them from oak because I had offcuts. The offset is 1/16" which should draw the joint tight. First time doing drawboring. Watched the Peter Follansbee video four times.

Mistake report: Chopped the first mortise about 1/4" too far to one side. The leg will be offset slightly. I'm telling myself it adds character. It does not add character. It adds regret.

The dog sat in my lap while I was chopping mortises. She weighs 60 pounds. This was not ergonomic.
09 — assembly day

Putting It All Together

feb 22

Today is the day. All the pieces are done. The mortises are chopped. The tenons are fitted. The drawbore holes are drilled. The pegs are whittled. I have glue, a mallet, and a deep sense of anxiety.

Assembly diagram — base frame

Assembly went... OK. The short end assemblies went together perfectly. The long stretchers were a fight. One of the tenons was just a hair too tight and I had to persuade it with the mallet and some very targeted language. But everything pulled up tight with the drawbored pegs and it's SOLID. I can stand on it without any flex.

The base weighs approximately one million pounds. Moving it into position required bribing my neighbor with beer. The beer cost more than some of the lumber.

Now I just need to get the top onto the base, install the vise, drill the dog holes, and apply finish. Just those things. Simple. (narrator: it was not simple)

Finish

Going with a mix of boiled linseed oil and paste wax. The BLO soaks into the SYP beautifully and the wax gives it a nice feel. Three coats of BLO, 24 hours between coats, then paste wax buffed with a rag. The bench will darken over time and that's exactly what I want.

A note on bench finish: Don't use poly on a workbench. I know it's tempting. But poly will chip off under use and then you have a bench with patchy, flaking finish. BLO + wax is renewable — just add more when it looks dry. Chris Schwarz has a whole rant about this and he's right. He's always right about bench stuff.

11 — final thoughts

Was It Worth It?

feb 23 — done!!!

Yes. Absolutely yes. The new bench is everything the old one wasn't. It's flat, it's heavy, the vise works properly, the dog holes are (mostly) on a grid, and it doesn't have a pizza box shim. These are significant improvements.

1

Total Cost

~$450 ~$510 (forgot about the combination square, the extra glue, and the beer for the neighbor). Still way cheaper than buying a comparable bench, which would be $1200+ easy.

2

Total Time

About 4 weekends plus some evening sessions. Maybe 50 hours of actual work? Hard to say because a lot of that was watching YouTube videos and calling it "research."

3

Mistakes Made

Off-center mortise, wrong stretcher length (fixed), Plan A for the milling (abandoned), the Great Clamp Disaster of Feb 15, and one dog hole that's 1/4" off the grid. Acceptable losses.

4

Things Learned

Drawboring is amazing and I'm using it on everything from now on. SYP is an underrated bench wood. You ALWAYS need more clamps. The dog will always be in the way. Fleetwood Mac is ideal planing music.

The dog has claimed the space under the new bench as her spot. She fits perfectly. This was not planned but it's the best feature of the whole build.
Next project on the new bench: a set of sawhorses. Then a tool chest. Then maybe that dining table my wife has been asking about for two years. The bench was step one. Everything else follows.

Onward.
