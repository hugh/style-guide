my notes on building the

Walnut Bookcase

I've been meaning to build this thing for about three years. Finally pulled the trigger after finding a beautiful 8/4 walnut slab at the mill. These are my build notes — mistakes and all.

started jan 2026 · still going
01 — the wood

Lumber Selection

I drove out to Anderson's mill on a Saturday. They had a fresh batch of 8/4 walnut — some really nice stuff, but also plenty of boards with too much sapwood for what I wanted.

Ended up buying about 40 board feet, which should be more than enough. Famous last words.

Always buy 20% more than you think you need. I learned this the hard way on the dining table.

What I Got

🌲

8/4 Walnut

Two gorgeous boards for the sides. Straight grain, minimal sapwood, about 10" wide each. These were the best ones at the mill.

🍁

4/4 Walnut

For shelves and the back boards. Doesn't need to be as pretty since the back gets shiplapped. Some character is fine.

🍒

Scraps Bin

Grabbed a few cherry offcuts for wedges and pegs. Contrast looks nice against the walnut. $12 well spent

🔧

Hardware

1/4" brass shelf pins, some Titebond III, and a new pack of 150-grit. Also finally bought that Veritas dovetail saw I've been eyeing.

Note to self: Let this stuff sit in the shop for at least a week before milling. The moisture content at Anderson's is always a little high. Last time I rushed it and everything cupped learned an expensive lesson.

03 — the drawings

Dimensions

I sketched this up on graph paper first, then made a quick SketchUp model. The proportions are based on a bookcase in Tage Frid's book that I've always liked.

Sketch 01 — Main Carcase

Overall Dimensions

Traditional proportions. The height-to-width ratio is roughly 4:3, which looks right to my eye. Might go an inch taller, we'll see.

36″ wide 48″ tall 12″ deep sides: 3/4″
Sketch 02 — Shelf Pins

Adjustable Shelves

Brass shelf pins on 1-1/2" centers. I'm going to use a simple drilling jig made from a scrap of plywood and a bushing. Did this on the last project and it worked great.

1/4″ pin holes 1/2″ deep 1-1/2″ spacing

Update: went with 2" spacing instead. More holes than I need but gives more flexibility later.

04 — the joints

Joinery

Through dovetails for the corners, dadoes for the fixed shelf, rabbets for the back. Nothing fancy, but all hand-cut because that's the point.

1

Through Dovetails

For the four corners. I like a 1:6 ratio for hardwood. Marking with a wheel gauge and cutting with the new Veritas saw. Should be satisfying.

2

Dados for Fixed Shelf

Stopped dados through dados — changed my mind. Through is easier and the shelf covers the joint from the front anyway.

3

Rabbets for Back

Shiplapped back boards sit in a rabbet on the inside of the carcase. Allows for seasonal wood movement. Learned this from Chris Schwarz's blog.

06 — assembly

Putting It Together

Dry fit everything before glue-up. I cannot stress this enough. The first time I tried assembling a dovetailed carcase with glue, I ran out of open time and it was a disaster.

Rough sketch — Carcase Assembly

Not to scale, obviously. But you get the idea.

Finish

Going with Osmo Polyx-Oil in satin. It's what I used on the dining table and it's held up beautifully over two years. Three coats, sanding lightly with 400-grit between coats.
