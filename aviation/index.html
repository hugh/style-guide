03 — Layout

Structure

Documents use a single-column layout with a centered container. Sections stack vertically with generous spacing and instrument-panel separators against the dark cockpit background.

Max-width 900px, centered, with 2rem horizontal padding.

⚙️

Sections

5rem vertical padding. Separated by 1px solid border-top.

↔️

Full-Bleed

Diagrams can break out to 1100px max-width with the full-bleed class.

📱

Responsive

Fluid hero type with clamp(). Card grids collapse to 1 column below 640px.

Section Pattern

Every section follows the same structure: a monospace readout label in the format 01 — Label, followed by an h2 heading, then body content and components.

06 — Key Patterns

Design Principles

These patterns define the visual identity of the Aviation / Cockpit system.

1

Instrument Panel Dark

Backgrounds use cold grey-blue near-blacks, evoking an unlit cockpit at night. Never pure #000 — always slightly warm or cool-tinted.

2

Amber Instrument Accent

The primary accent is warm instrument amber, like classic cockpit gauge backlighting. It marks headings, callout borders, and principle numbers.

3

Aviation Semantics

Four semantic colors map to flight concepts: phosphor green (go/cleared), caution amber (advisory/hold), warning red (alert/emergency), navigation blue (heading/route).

4

Condensed Cockpit Headings

Barlow Condensed delivers tight uppercase headings that echo the instrument panel labels found on real aircraft dashboards.

5

Monospace Readouts

IBM Plex Mono gives all labels, tags, and data the feel of cockpit instrument readouts and flight management computer displays.

6

Instrument Gauge Motifs

Altimeter dials, artificial horizons, radio logs, and black box recorders bring real cockpit instruments into the design language.

7

Subtle Cockpit Glow

The body has a faint radial overlay mixing amber and green to simulate dim cockpit lighting during night operations.
